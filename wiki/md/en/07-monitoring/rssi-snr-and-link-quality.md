# RSSI, SNR, and Radio-Link Quality

RSSI, noise floor, and SNR measure different properties of a received signal. No single value describes the reliability of a MeshCore route. Engineering conclusions require packet series, Packet Delivery Ratio, ACK results, and behavior over time.

![RSSI, noise floor, and SNR](/attachments/en/signal-metrics.svg?v=2)

## RSSI

**Received Signal Strength Indicator** estimates the power of a received signal at the receiver input. It is usually expressed in dBm and is normally negative.

Relative examples:

```text
-60 dBm is stronger than -90 dBm
-90 dBm is stronger than -120 dBm
```

A 30 dB difference corresponds to a thousandfold power ratio.

Packet RSSI depends on:

- TX power;
- antenna gain and polarization;
- path loss;
- fading;
- bandwidth and receiver implementation;
- chip calibration offset;
- strong in-band interference;
- measurement timing.

RSSI cannot be converted directly into distance without an environmental model.

## Packet RSSI and channel RSSI

**Packet RSSI** is estimated while receiving a frame. **Channel RSSI** measures current channel energy without requiring a valid packet.

High channel RSSI with no valid packets often indicates:

- wideband noise;
- another transmitter;
- receiver overload;
- digital noise generated by the board itself;
- incorrect calibration.

MeshCore `stats-radio` reports noise floor and last RSSI/SNR, while raw logging can store values for every packet.

## Noise floor

Noise floor is the background against which the wanted signal must be detected. It includes:

- thermal noise;
- receiver noise figure;
- local electronics;
- external RF emitters;
- bandwidth;
- gain state.

Theoretical thermal-noise density is about `−174 dBm/Hz` at room temperature. Across a bandwidth it becomes approximately:

```text
Noise ≈ -174 + 10·log10(BW_Hz) + NoiseFigure
```

This is a reference value. Real urban noise can be much higher.

Doubling BW increases integrated noise power by roughly 3 dB.

## SNR

**Signal-to-Noise Ratio** is:

```text
SNR(dB) = Signal(dBm) - Noise(dBm)
```

Because of processing gain, LoRa can decode packets at negative SNR. The minimum usable SNR depends on SF, BW, CR, and the radio chip.

Example:

```text
signal = -120 dBm
noise  = -110 dBm
SNR    = -10 dB
```

The signal is weaker than the total noise power, yet a LoRa packet may still decode.

## SNR ×4 in MeshCore

Inside `Packet`, SNR is stored as signed `int8` in quarter-decibel units:

```text
stored = SNR · 4
SNR = stored / 4
```

Companion frames and neighbor output use the same convention. A parser must convert byte values `0..255` to signed `−128..127` before dividing.

## Why strong RSSI can be bad

Example:

```text
RSSI = -70 dBm
SNR  = -12 dB
```

The total received energy is strong, but most of it is interference. The packet is embedded in a noisy environment.

Another example:

```text
RSSI = -120 dBm
SNR  = +5 dB
```

The signal is weak, but the channel is very quiet. This link may be more stable than the first as long as adequate fade margin remains.

## Sensitivity and required SNR

Receiver sensitivity can be approximated as:

```text
Sensitivity ≈ thermal noise + 10log10(BW) + NF + required SNR
```

A higher SF permits a lower required SNR. Therefore, the useful RSSI threshold changes with the radio profile. A generic table saying “−120 dBm is good or bad” is meaningless without SF and BW.

## Packet Delivery Ratio

```text
PDR = received_unique / sent
```

PDR is the main practical metric. Count unique packets and exclude retries and duplicates, or state the methodology explicitly.

Example:

- 100 test messages sent;
- destination received 92 unique messages;
- PDR = 92%;
- sender received 85 ACKs;
- end-to-end confirmed ratio = 85%.

The difference points to reverse-path or ACK problems.

## Packet Error Rate

PER is the fraction of packets that are erroneous or not received. A radio usually cannot count every lost packet because it does not know how many were transmitted. A test payload needs a sequence counter.

A CRC-error counter includes only frames where the receiver detected a candidate and found a CRC mismatch. A completely undetected preamble is not counted.

## SNR link margin

If the profile requires `SNRmin`, the margin is:

```text
SNR margin = measured SNR - SNRmin
```

Required SNR must come from the data sheet for the relevant conditions. Margin must cover fading and interference. Median margin alone is insufficient; minimum and low percentiles matter.

## Route-level metrics

A route contains several hops. End-to-end PDR approximately multiplies individual link success probabilities:

```text
Proute ≈ P1 · P2 · ... · Ph
```

If each of five hops succeeds 95% of the time:

```text
0.95^5 ≈ 77%
```

The ACK must travel back as well, so confirmed success can be lower still. Many “fairly good” hops can therefore create a noticeably poor end-to-end route.

## Use a series, not one packet

A minimum test should include:

- 50–100 packets;
- sequence number;
- identical packet size;
- randomized interval;
- RSSI and SNR for each reception;
- duplicates;
- latency;
- ACK status;
- time and radio profile.

Calculate:

- median RSSI/SNR;
- 10th percentile;
- PDR;
- confirmed ratio;
- duplicate ratio;
- longest outage duration.

## Packet-length effect

A longer packet has more airtime and a larger chance of overlap. At the same RSSI, long-payload PDR may be worse.

A successful short advert does not guarantee delivery of a maximum-size text or group datagram. Test realistic lengths.

## Differences between radio chips

SX127x, SX126x, LR1110, and their wrappers may use different RSSI offsets and algorithms. Compare absolute values from different boards cautiously.

For relative testing, keep one receiver and change one factor. For fleet monitoring, calibrate models against a common signal source first.

## Problem patterns

| Observation | Possible cause |
|---|---|
| RSSI and SNR gradually decline | Cable, moisture, foliage, movement |
| High RSSI, low SNR | Interference or overload |
| Good RSSI/SNR, poor PDR | Collisions, queueing, firmware, hash collision |
| Direct poor, flood good | Stale path or one bad hop |
| Data arrives, ACK does not | Reverse asymmetry |
| Reading changes when touched | Antenna or ground-plane issue |

## UI presentation

Do not compress quality into one signal bar. Useful displays include:

- last RSSI/SNR with timestamp;
- sample count;
- PDR window;
- route hop count;
- ACK ratio;
- radio profile;
- warning when the sample is stale.
